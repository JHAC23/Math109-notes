Section 01
01 · Definition

What Is a Higher-Degree Polynomial?

A polynomial is an expression built from constants and non-negative integer powers of x combined by addition and subtraction. The degree is simply the highest power of x that appears.

Key Definition

A higher-degree polynomial function is any polynomial whose degree is greater than 2 (i.e., cubic, quartic, or higher).

General form: f(x) = a_n x^n + a_(n-1) x^(n-1) + ... + a_1 x + a_0

Cubic
Degree 3

ax^3 + bx^2 + cx + d
where a ≠ 0

Quartic
Degree 4

ax^4 + bx^3 + cx^2 + dx + e
where a ≠ 0

⚠️
Watch out: Polynomials are not always written in descending order. The degree is the highest power of x — not necessarily the first term you see. Example: 5x + 3x^3 has degree 3.
Section 02
02 · Graphs

Cubics & Quartics

Cubic — degree 3
At most 2 turning points

Can have 0 turning points (always increasing or always decreasing) or 2 turning points (the classic S/hump shape).

Quartic — degree 4
At most 3 turning points

Can have 1 turning point (parabola-like) or 3 turning points (W or M shape).

Section 03
03 · Turning Points

Turning Points & Local Extrema

A turning point is where the graph changes direction — from increasing to decreasing, or vice versa. The formal math term is local (relative) extrema.

Local Minimum
Decreasing → Increasing

The graph goes down, hits the lowest point in that area, then turns and goes back up.

Local Maximum
Increasing → Decreasing

The graph goes up, hits the highest point in that area, then turns and goes back down.

🔑
Slowing down ≠ turning point. If the graph slows down but never reverses direction, it is NOT a turning point. It must actually change direction.
TI-83/84 Calculator Tip

Graph the function, then press 2nd → CALC and choose minimum or maximum. Set a left bound, right bound, and guess — the calculator returns the exact coordinates.

Example Identify x-intercepts and local extrema from a graph

x-intercepts (A) x = 0 and x = 3 Graph touches/crosses the x-axis at these two values

Section 04
04 · Absolute Extrema

Absolute vs. Relative Extrema

Absolute Maximum
Highest y-value on the interval

The tallest point over the entire interval being examined. May or may not coincide with a relative maximum.

Absolute Minimum
Lowest y-value on the interval

The lowest point over the entire interval. May or may not coincide with a relative minimum.

Important Distinction

A relative max/min can also be an absolute max/min — but it doesn't have to be. Always check the endpoints of the interval and compare all values.

Example from Dr. Jenkins' slides: Relative max at (-0.408, 0.272), Relative min at (0.408, -0.272). But the absolute max on that interval is the endpoint (1, 1), and the absolute min is the relative min (0.408, -0.272).

Section 05
05 · End Behavior

End Behavior

End behavior describes what happens to the graph on the far left (as x → −∞) and the far right (as x → +∞). For each side, the graph is either rising (going up) or falling (going down).

  • If the graph goes UP to the left → Rises left
  • If the graph goes DOWN to the left → Falls left
  • If the graph goes UP to the right → Rises right
  • If the graph goes DOWN to the right → Falls right
✏️
No graph? No problem. You can determine end behavior from the equation alone using the Leading Coefficient Test in the next section.
Section 06
06 · LCT

The Leading Coefficient Test (LCT)

The leading coefficient is the number attached to the highest-power x term (not necessarily the first term in the expression).

The Two Questions to Ask

1. Is the degree (n) even or odd?

2. Is the leading coefficient positive or negative?

Degree Positive Leading Coeff. Negative Leading Coeff.
Even (2, 4, 6…) Rises left AND rises right Falls left AND falls right
Odd (1, 3, 5…) Falls left, rises right Rises left, falls right
Memory Trick — Even
Same direction on both ends

Think of x^2 (parabola): both ends go the same way. Positive → both up. Negative → both down.

Memory Trick — Odd
Opposite directions on each end

Think of x^3: the two ends always go in opposite directions. Positive → falls left, rises right.

Section 08
08 · Reading a Graph

Putting It All Together

From any graph, you should be able to state five things. Use this checklist for every graph question on tests and homework.

Number of x-intercepts
Number of turning points
LC positive or negative
Degree even or odd
Minimum possible degree
Minimum Possible Degree — How to Find It

Turning points ≤ n−1, so: min degree = (# of turning points) + 1

BUT also: it must be at least as big as the number of x-intercepts, AND must match the parity (even/odd) determined by end behavior.

Take the larger of the two constraints, adjusted for parity.

Examples 1–4 Graph Analysis Walkthrough
GraphKey FactsConclusion
Ex 1 2 x-intercepts, 1 turning point, rises both ends Even degree, positive LC, min degree = 2
Ex 2 3 x-intercepts (one just touches), 3 turning points, falls both ends Even degree, negative LC, min degree = 4 (not 3 — needs even degree with 3 turning points → n−1=3 → n=4)
Ex 3 1 x-intercept, 0 turning points, rises left / falls right Odd degree, negative LC, min degree = 3 (not 1 — not a straight line!)
Ex 4 3 x-intercepts, 2 turning points, falls left / rises right Odd degree, positive LC, min degree = 3
📝
Test tip: For these "putting it all together" problems, show your reasoning step by step — identify end behavior first (to get even/odd and LC sign), then count intercepts and turning points, then determine min degree.
