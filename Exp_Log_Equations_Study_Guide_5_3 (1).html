Section 01

The Big Idea — What Are We Doing Here?

In Section 5.1, you learned about exponential functions (the variable is in the exponent). In Section 5.2, you learned about logarithms (the inverse of exponentials).

Now in 5.3, we put them together to solve equations. You'll see two types of problems:

Type 1: Exponential Equations

The variable is stuck in an exponent and you need to get it down.

32x = 81    or    150 · 104t = 3000

Tool to solve: Use logarithms to bring the variable down from the exponent.

Type 2: Logarithmic Equations

The variable is trapped inside a logarithm and you need to free it.

6 + 3 · ln(x) = 12    or    log(x+3) = 2

Tool to solve: Convert to exponential form to undo the log.

💡 The Core Concept: Logs and exponentials are inverses — they undo each other. If the variable is in an exponent, use a log to bring it down. If the variable is in a log, use an exponential to pop it out. That's this entire section.
Section 02

Method 1: Solving Exponentials Using Log Form

🗒️ The Steps
  1. Isolate the exponential. Get the "base raised to a power" piece completely alone on one side.
  2. Rewrite in log form. Use the swoop from Section 5.2: if ay = x, then loga(x) = y.
  3. Solve for the variable. Use algebra to finish.
  4. Use calculator if needed. If you end up with a common log or natural log, plug it in.
Full Worked Example

Solve for t:   3000 = 150 · 104t   (round to 3 decimal places)

Given:
3000 = 150 · 104t
Step 1:
3000 / 150 = 104t
divide both sides by 150
Simplify:
20 = 104t
3000 / 150 = 20
Step 2:
log10(20) = 4t
rewrite in log form (swoop!)
Simplify:
log(20) = 4t
log10 is just "log" on calculator
Step 3:
t = log(20) / 4
divide both sides by 4
Calculator:
t = 1.301 / 4 = 0.325
press LOG, type 20, enter

We chose log base 10 here because the base in the problem was 10 — it matched nicely. But as we'll see, you can always use ln instead if you prefer.

💡 Why did we choose log base 10?

Because the equation had 104t — the base was already 10. When you take log10 of 10something, the base-10 and the log-base-10 cancel out (Property 3 from 5.2), and you're just left with the exponent. That's the whole trick!

Section 03

The Change-of-Base Formula

Sometimes you end up with a log that has a weird base — like log3(17) or log0.5(something). Your calculator can't do those directly. The Change-of-Base Formula converts ANY log into one your calculator can handle.

📜 The Formula
loga(x) = log(x) / log(a) = ln(x) / ln(a)

Pick whichever you prefer — common log (log) or natural log (ln). Both give the same answer!

💡 In Plain English: To compute a log with any base, just divide: put the number inside the log on top, and the base on the bottom. Use either the LOG or LN button for both parts — just be consistent.
Example 1 — log3(17)

Can't type log base 3 into calculator. Use change of base:

Formula:
log3(17) = ln(17) / ln(3)
Calculator:
= 2.833 / 1.099 = 2.579

You'd get the same answer using log(17)/log(3). Try both and see!

Example 2 — log6(2)
Formula:
log6(2) = ln(2) / ln(6)
Calculator:
= 0.693 / 1.792 = 0.387

Real-World Example: Radiocarbon Dating

Half-Life Problem

A fossil originally had 12g of radiocarbon and now has 0.2g. The half-life is 5730 years. How old is it?

Half-life formula: P(t) = P0 · (1/2)t/k

where P0 = original amount, P(t) = current amount, k = half-life

Plug in:
0.2 = 12 · (0.5)t/5730
Divide by 12:
0.2/12 = (0.5)t/5730
Simplify:
0.01667 = (0.5)t/5730
Log form:
log0.5(0.01667) = t/5730
swoop!
Change base:
ln(0.01667) / ln(0.5) = t/5730
can't type log base 0.5
Calculator:
(-4.094) / (-0.693) = t/5730
Simplify:
5.907 = t/5730
Multiply:
t = 5730 · 5.907
Answer:
t ≈ 33,846 years

Watch those parentheses in your calculator! This is a problem where one misplaced parenthesis can throw off everything.

Section 04

Method 2: Solving Exponentials Using Log Properties

This is your instructor's preferred method and honestly the most versatile one. Instead of rewriting in log form, you just take the ln (or log) of BOTH sides and use the Power Rule to bring the exponent down.

🗒️ The Steps
  1. Isolate the exponential. Get the base-to-a-power alone on one side.
  2. Take ln (or log) of BOTH sides. Whatever you do to one side, do to the other.
  3. Use the Power Rule to pull the exponent down in front: ln(ax) = x · ln(a).
  4. Solve for the variable using basic algebra.
💡 Why is This the Preferred Method?

Because it works every time, no matter what the base is. You don't need to match bases, you don't need to think about swooping — you just slap ln on both sides and use the Power Rule. It's mechanical and reliable.

Example 1 — Solve: 33x = 19683
Given:
33x = 19683
already isolated
Step 2:
ln(33x) = ln(19683)
take ln of both sides
Step 3:
3x · ln(3) = ln(19683)
Power Rule: exponent drops down
Step 4:
3x = ln(19683) / ln(3)
divide both sides by ln(3)
Simplify:
3x = 9.889 / 1.099 = 9
calculator
Answer:
x = 9 / 3 = 3
divide by 3
Example 2 — Solve: 5 · (0.3)3x+7 = 30

This one has more algebra. Take it slow.

Given:
5 · (0.3)3x+7 = 30
Step 1:
(0.3)3x+7 = 6
divide both sides by 5
Step 2:
ln(0.33x+7) = ln(6)
take ln of both sides
Step 3:
(3x + 7) · ln(0.3) = ln(6)
Power Rule
Divide:
3x + 7 = ln(6) / ln(0.3)
divide by ln(0.3)
Calculator:
3x + 7 = 1.792 / (-1.204)
Simplify:
3x + 7 = -1.488
Subtract 7:
3x = -1.488 - 7 = -8.488
Answer:
x = -8.488 / 3 = -2.829

Notice: after the Power Rule, it was just basic algebra — subtract, divide. The log part is only 2 steps, then you're back to algebra you already know.

⚠️ Common Pitfall: Rounding Too Early

If you solve ln(6)/ln(0.3) and get a decimal, carry extra decimal places through the rest of your algebra. If you round too early, the error accumulates and your final answer can be wrong. MyLab is especially picky about this! Keep at least 4-5 decimal places until the very end.

Section 05

Method 3: Solving Log Equations Using Exponentials

Now we flip it around. If the variable is inside a logarithm, we use the inverse idea: convert to exponential form to free the variable.

🗒️ The Steps
  1. Isolate the logarithm. Get the log term alone on one side.
  2. Convert to exponential form. Use the swoop: if loga(x) = y, then ay = x.
  3. Solve for the variable using algebra.
Example — Solve: 6 + 3 · ln(x) = 12
Given:
6 + 3 · ln(x) = 12
Subtract 6:
3 · ln(x) = 6
Divide by 3:
ln(x) = 2
log is now isolated!
Think:
loge(x) = 2
ln means log base e
Exponential:
e2 = x
swoop! base e, exponent 2
Calculator:
x = e2 ≈ 7.389
press 2nd → ex → 2
💡 The Key Move

When you see ln(something) = number, raise e to both sides:

ln(x) = 2   →   eln(x) = e2   →   x = e2

The e and ln cancel each other (they're inverses!), freeing whatever was inside the ln.

Same idea with common log: if log(x) = 3, then 103 = x = 1000.

Section 06

Method 4: Solving Log Equations Using Log Properties

Sometimes the equation has multiple log terms. First, use the properties from 5.2 (Product, Quotient, Power) to combine them into a single log, THEN convert to exponential form.

🗒️ The Steps
  1. Get all log terms on one side and everything else on the other.
  2. Use log properties to combine into a SINGLE logarithm (Quotient Rule, Product Rule, etc.).
  3. Convert to exponential form to eliminate the log.
  4. Solve for x with algebra.
Example — Solve: ln(x) + 3 = ln(x + 4)

This one has TWO ln terms. Let's handle it step by step.

Given:
ln(x) + 3 = ln(x + 4)
Step 1:
3 = ln(x + 4) - ln(x)
move ln(x) to the right
Step 2:
3 = ln((x + 4) / x)
Quotient Rule! subtraction → division
Step 3:
e3 = (x + 4) / x
convert to exponential form
Multiply by x:
x · e3 = x + 4
get rid of fraction
Rearrange:
x · e3 - x = 4
move x terms to one side
Factor:
x(e3 - 1) = 4
factor out x
Divide:
x = 4 / (e3 - 1)
Calculator:
x = 4 / (20.086 - 1) = 4 / 19.086 ≈ 0.210

Notice the pattern: combine the logs → convert to exponential → pure algebra. The log stuff disappears after step 3, and you're just doing algebra you already know.

⚠️ Reminder from 5.2

ln(x + 4) is NOT the same as ln(x) + ln(4)!

You can only combine logs with the Quotient/Product Rule when logs are being added or subtracted from each other. The stuff INSIDE a single log cannot be split by addition.

Section 07

How to Pick Your Method (Decision Guide)

When you see a problem, ask yourself these questions in order:

Is the variable in an exponent?
→ Yes? You have an exponential equation. Use Method 2 (take ln of both sides, Power Rule, solve). This works every time.
Is the variable inside a log?
→ Yes? You have a log equation. If there's ONE log term, isolate it and convert to exponential form (Method 3). If there are MULTIPLE log terms, combine them first using properties (Method 4), then convert.
Do you have a weird base you can't type in?
→ Use the Change-of-Base Formula to convert it to ln or log that your calculator can handle.
ln or log — which should I use?
→ Either one! They give the same answer. Your instructor tends to use ln. Pick your favorite and stick with it.
Section 08

Checking Your Work on the TI-84

Your instructor showed this in the lecture — it's a great way to verify answers.

💻 The Intersect Method
  1. Put the left side of the equation into Y1. Put the right side into Y2.
  2. Press GRAPH to see both curves. (Adjust your window if needed.)
  3. Press 2nd → CALC → 5:intersect.
  4. Follow the prompts (first curve? second curve? guess?) and the calculator gives you the x-value where they meet. That x-value is your answer!
Example: Checking 6 + 3·ln(x) = 12

Y1 = 6 + 3·ln(X)

Y2 = 12

Graph both, find intersection → x ≈ 7.389 ✓

This works for ANY equation — exponential or logarithmic. It's also a lifesaver if you get stuck algebraically: just graph both sides and find the intersection.
